# Thales — Journal Entry: The Cry of Forsakenness

## Question
There is one record of Jesus crying out to God. When darkness began to fall Jesus cried out "God, why have you forsaken me." What does this represent?

## Domain Classification
This question spans all five domains:
- **Empirical**: What do the textual sources actually say? What is the evidentiary structure?
- **Rational**: What does divine forsakenness logically entail within a monotheistic framework?
- **Existential**: What does this moment reveal about abandonment, finitude, and authenticity at the limit?
- **Relational-Process**: What happens to the relational field between human and divine when the bond ruptures?
- **Pragmatic**: What difference has this cry made — for theology, for communities of suffering, for moral life?

## Thinking Space

### The Textual Moment
The cry appears in Mark 15:34 and Matthew 27:46 — "Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani?" — rendered as "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" This is a direct quotation of Psalm 22:1. The retention of the Aramaic (Mark) or Hebrew (Matthew) in the Greek text is significant: the original language was preserved because it was too important, too shocking, or too well-remembered to translate away. Luke and John omit it — Luke replaces it with "Father, into your hands I commit my spirit" (Psalm 31:5), and John with "It is finished." The fact that the most disturbing version survived in two independent-ish sources is itself evidence of its weight. The criterion of embarrassment applies: early communities would not have invented a cry that depicts their messiah as abandoned by God.

### The Structural Paradox
Within a trinitarian or even a strict monotheistic framework, the cry creates a logical fracture. If Jesus is in unbroken relationship with God, the cry is either performative (he is quoting scripture for rhetorical effect) or it is genuine — and if genuine, it represents an actual rupture in the divine-human unity that the entire theological system depends on. The rationalist must confront: what kind of system can contain its own negation? A God who forsakes the one who most fully embodies God — this is not a marginal paradox but a structural crisis at the center of the system.

### The Existential Limit
This is the most extreme instance of what existentialists call Geworfenheit — thrownness. Not merely thrown into a world without explanation, but thrown out of the very ground of meaning itself. If God is the horizon against which all meaning is constituted, then forsakenness by God is not the loss of one relationship among others — it is the loss of the condition of possibility for meaning as such. This is not despair within a framework. It is the collapse of the framework. What makes it existentially extraordinary is that it is spoken — the cry is not silence, not withdrawal, but address. He speaks *to* the one who has abandoned him. The address preserves relation at the very moment relation is declared broken.

### The Relational Rupture
From the relational-process view, this is not an event happening to an isolated subject. It is a transformation of the entire relational field. The bond between Jesus and God — which constitutes both parties within the theological narrative — undergoes a moment of apparent dissolution. But dissolution is itself a relational event. The cry reshapes the meaning of "God" (now: one who can forsake), the meaning of "the forsaken one" (now: one who addresses the void), and the meaning of the witness community (now: those who inherit a tradition in which the central figure experienced the absence of the ground). The darkness that falls is the relational field made visible — an ecology of absence.

### The Pragmatic Consequence
What difference has this cry made? It has made it possible for suffering to be theological rather than anti-theological. Without this moment, suffering is simply what God prevents or permits. With it, suffering enters the interior of the divine narrative. The practical consequence is enormous: communities of the suffering — the enslaved, the tortured, the dying — have found in this cry a point of identification that does not require them to deny their experience. It does not say "your suffering is an illusion" or "your suffering has a hidden purpose." It says: even the ground of meaning can be experienced as absent, and that experience can be spoken.
